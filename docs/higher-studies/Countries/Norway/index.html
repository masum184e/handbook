Norway

Norwegian universities are globally respected, many programs are taught in English, and public universities have traditionally offered tuition-free education, making Norway a unique option for students who want quality education with long-term career value.

Why Study in Norway from Bangladesh​

Studying in Norway offers many academic and lifestyle benefits for Bangladeshi students.

  • World-Class Education System
    • Publicly funded, high-quality education
    • Strong focus on critical thinking and research
    • Modern teaching methods and facilities
  • Global Recognition of Norwegian Degrees
    • Degrees are recognized worldwide
    • Strong academic reputation across Europe and beyond
  • English-Taught Programs
    • Most Master’s programs are taught fully in English
    • Some Bachelor’s programs available in English
  • Strong Research and Innovation Environment Norway is a leader in:
    • Renewable energy
    • Environmental science
    • Marine and Arctic research
    • Technology and data science
  • High Quality of Life and Safety
    • One of the safest countries in the world
    • Clean environment and low crime rate
  • Excellent Work-Life Balance
    • Student-friendly academic culture
    • Healthy balance between study, work, and personal life

Step-by-Step Process to Study in Norway from Bangladesh​

Step 1: Research Courses & Universities​

Start by selecting the right course and university.

Public vs Private Universities

  • Public universities: Traditionally tuition-free or low tuition
  • Private universities: Charge tuition fees

Popular Fields for Bangladeshi Students

  • Engineering
  • Computer Science & IT
  • Data Science & Artificial Intelligence
  • Environmental Studies
  • Marine Science
  • Business & Management

Language of Instruction

  • Master’s level: Mostly English
  • Bachelor’s level: Limited English programs, many taught in Norwegian

Step 2: Check Eligibility Requirements​

Academic Qualifications

  • Bachelor’s: SSC + HSC with strong academic background
  • Master’s: Recognized Bachelor’s degree (usually 4 years)
  • Diploma programs: Varies by institution

English Language Requirements

Typical score ranges:

  • IELTS: Overall 6.0–6.5 (no band below 5.5 or 6.0)
  • TOEFL iBT: 80–90+

(Some universities may waive IELTS if previous education was in English, but this is rare.)

Work Experience

  • Required for some specialized or executive programs
  • Not mandatory for most academic programs

Step 3: Prepare Required Documents​

Commonly required documents include:

  • Academic transcripts and certificates
  • Valid passport
  • Updated CV (Europass format preferred)
  • Statement of Purpose (SOP)
  • Letters of Recommendation (1–2)
  • English language test results
  • Portfolio (for design-related programs)

Step 4: Apply to Norwegian Universities​

Application Deadlines

  • Usually December to March
  • Some programs close earlier

Application Portals

  • University websites
  • Samordna Opptak (mainly for Bachelor’s programs)

Application Fees

  • Many public universities do not charge application fees
  • Some private institutions may charge a small fee

Step 5: Receive Offer Letter​

Types of offers:

  • Conditional Offer: Additional documents or results required
  • Unconditional Offer: All requirements met

After receiving the offer:

  • Accept the offer within the given deadline
  • Prepare for the residence permit application

Step 6: Pay Tuition Fee (If Applicable)​

  • Many public universities have low or no tuition
  • Some programs may charge fees for non-EU students

Payment Methods

  • International bank transfer
  • Online payment systems (if available)

Step 7: Apply for Norway Student Residence Permit from Bangladesh​

Bangladeshi students apply for a Student Residence Permit.

Required Documents

  • Offer letter
  • Passport
  • Proof of accommodation
  • Proof of funds (bank statement or sponsor)
  • Tuition payment receipt (if applicable)
  • Application form and photos

Proof of Funds

  • Approx. NOK 137,907 per year (subject to updates)
  • Must be shown in a bank account or financial guarantee

Processing Time

  • Usually 4–8 weeks

Step 8: Accommodation & Living Arrangements​

University Housing

  • Affordable and convenient
  • Limited availability

Private Accommodation

  • Shared apartments are common
  • Rent varies by city

Average Rent

  • NOK 4,000–7,000 per month (shared)
  • NOK 7,000–10,000+ (single studio)

Step 9: Pre-Departure Preparation​

Checklist:

  • Book flights early
  • Arrange health insurance
  • Exchange currency
  • Prepare warm clothing
  • Carry original academic documents

Step 10: Arrival in Norway & Post-Arrival Steps​

After arrival:

  • Register with the local police
  • Collect residence card
  • Complete university registration
  • Open a Norwegian bank account
  • Register for a personal ID number (if applicable)

Pros and Cons of Studying in Norway​

Pros

  • High-quality, research-driven education
  • Tuition-free or low-cost public universities
  • Safe and clean environment
  • Strong sustainability focus

Cons

  • High living costs
  • Limited part-time job availability
  • Cold climate, especially in winter

Scholarships for Bangladeshi Students in Norway​

Available Options

  • Norwegian Government Scholarships
  • Erasmus+
  • University-specific scholarships

Funding Types

  • Partial tuition waiver
  • Living cost support
  • Full funding (very competitive)

Part-Time Work Opportunities​

  • Allowed 20 hours per week during study
  • Full-time during holidays

Common Jobs

  • Restaurant and café staff
  • Cleaning services
  • Warehouse work
  • University assistant roles

Average Hourly Wage

  • NOK 170–220 per hour

Post-Study Work & Career Opportunities​

Stay-Back Options

  • Students can apply for a job-seeker residence permit

Job Market Overview

Strong demand in:

  • IT & software development
  • Engineering
  • Renewable energy
  • Data analytics
  • Research and academia

Common Mistakes to Avoid​

  • Applying after deadlines
  • Submitting incomplete documents
  • Writing a weak SOP
  • Underestimating living expenses
  • Misunderstanding residence permit rules
