Analytical Writting

The GRE Analytical Writing section measures your ability to:

  • Analyze complex ideas
  • Evaluate arguments logically
  • Articulate reasoning clearly and coherently
  • Support claims with relevant examples
  • Write controlled, academic prose under time pressure

You are not graded on opinions or specialized knowledge. Like Quant and Verbal, AWA rewards:

Correct reasoning + clear structure + disciplined execution

Structure of the AWA Section

  • Issue Task: Present and defend a position on a general claim
  • Argument Task: Critically evaluate the logic of a given argument

Each task is scored 0–6 in half-point increments, using ETS’s published rubrics.

Issue Task — Constructing an Argument​

You are asked to take a position on a broadly stated issue and defend it using reasoning and examples.

This mirrors:

  • Quant: Choosing a method and justifying steps
  • Verbal (RC): Identifying a main claim and supporting it with evidence

ETS is testing:

  • Your ability to reason from principles
  • Your ability to qualify claims (not overgeneralize)
  • Your ability to organize ideas logically

Argument Task — Evaluating an Argument​

You are given a short argument and asked to analyze its logical flaws, not to agree or disagree with the conclusion.

This mirrors:

  • Quantitative Comparison: Testing assumptions by changing variables
  • Verbal (RC): Identifying unsupported inferences and weak evidence

ETS is testing:

  • Your ability to spot assumptions
  • Your ability to explain why reasoning is flawed
  • Your ability to propose what evidence would strengthen the argument

Skills Tested & Scoring Criteria​

High-Scoring (5–6) Responses Demonstrate:

SkillAWA EquivalentQuant / Verbal Parallel
Logical consistencyClaims follow from premisesAlgebraic validity
Evidence-based reasoningExamples support claimsData interpretation
PrecisionClear, qualified languageAnswer choice elimination
StructureClear paragraph rolesStep-by-step solutions
Critical evaluationIdentifying assumptionsRC inference questions

Important: Stylistic elegance is secondary. Logical clarity is primary.

Issue Task Strategy​

1. Developing a Position​

You may:

  • Agree
  • Disagree
  • Partially agree (often strongest)

High scorers avoid absolute claims unless the prompt explicitly demands them.

Quant Parallel: Just as “x > 0” limits conclusions in QC, real-world claims require conditions.

2. Structuring the Essay​

A strong Issue essay follows a logical progression, not a memorized template:

  1. Introduction
    • Rephrase the issue
    • State a clear, defensible thesis
    • Indicate scope (conditions or limits)
  2. Body Paragraphs (2–3)
    • One central claim per paragraph
    • Explanation → example → analysis
    • Explicit link back to thesis
  3. Counterargument (often embedded)
    • Acknowledge a reasonable objection
    • Explain why it is limited or conditional
  4. Conclusion
    • Reinforce thesis without repetition
    • Emphasize reasoning, not emotion

3. Using Examples Effectively​

Strong examples are:

  • Specific but not obscure
  • Historical, academic, institutional, or hypothetical
  • Directly tied to the claim

Weak examples:

  • Personal anecdotes
  • Vague references (“many people believe…”)

Verbal Parallel: Like RC questions, examples must directly support the claim, not merely relate to the topic.

Argument Task Strategy​

1. Identify the Conclusion and Evidence​

Before criticizing, map the argument:

  • What is the conclusion?
  • What evidence supports it?
  • What assumptions connect them?

This is equivalent to identifying:

  • Premise → inference → conclusion

2. Common Logical Flaws to Target​

FlawDescriptionQuant Analogy
Unstated assumptionMissing link between evidence and claimUndefined variable
Causation vs correlationAssumes cause without proofMisapplied formula
Sample biasUnrepresentative dataInvalid test case
OversimplificationIgnores alternativesIgnoring constraints

3. Suggest Improvements​

High-scoring responses do not stop at criticism. They explain:

  • What additional evidence is needed
  • What alternative explanations should be tested

Quant Parallel: Like checking multiple values in QC to confirm a relationship.

Logical Parallels to Quant and Verbal​

Quant → AWA

  • Define variables → define terms
  • Test boundary cases → consider counterexamples
  • Justify steps → explain reasoning

Verbal → AWA

  • Identify main idea → thesis
  • Evaluate evidence → analyze assumptions
  • Track structure → paragraph coherence

Key Insight: AWA is argument-based reasoning, not creative writing.

Step-by-Step Writing Framework​

Planning (1–2 Minutes)

  • Identify task type
  • Write:
    • Issue: thesis + 2 reasons
    • Argument: conclusion + 2–3 flaws

Writing (Main Time)

  • One idea per paragraph
  • Explicit logical transitions:
    • “This reasoning assumes…”
    • “However, this conclusion depends on…”

Revising (Final 1–2 Minutes)

Check:

  • Clear thesis
  • Topic sentences
  • Logical flow
  • Grammar and clarity

High-Scoring Techniques​

Thesis Construction

  • Specific, conditional, defensible
  • Avoid absolute claims unless justified

Cohesive Paragraphs

Each paragraph should answer:

What am I claiming, and why does it matter?

Transitions

Use logic-based transitions, not decorative ones:

  • “As a result…”
  • “This assumption fails because…”

Vocabulary & Syntax

  • Precise, not ornamental
  • Sentence variety without verbosity
  • Avoid memorized phrases
