Abstract

The abstract is a concise summary of the entire research pape## It allows readers, reviewers, and researchers to quickly understand what the study is about, how it was conducted, and what was found, without reading the full paper.

Purpose of an Abstract​

The abstract serves to:

  • Provide a brief overview of the research
  • Help readers decide whether the paper is relevant
  • Enable indexing and retrieval in academic databases
  • Summarize the key contribution of the study

In many cases, the abstract is the only part of the paper that is freely accessible.

Length and Placement​

  • Typically 150–250 words (depending on journal guidelines)
  • Written as one paragraph
  • Placed immediately after the title
  • Written last, after completing the paper

Key Components of an Abstract​

Most abstracts follow the IMRaD structure in condensed form:

Background / Problem Statement​

Briefly introduces the research problem or context.

Example: “With the rapid growth of online education, concerns have emerged regarding its impact on student learning outcomes.”

Objective / Purpose​

Clearly states the aim of the study.

Example: “This study aims to examine the effect of online learning on academic performance among university students.”

Methods​

Summarizes how the research was conducted.

Example: “A quantitative research design was employed, using survey data collected from 300 undergraduate students.”

Results / Findings​

Presents the key findings (often with limited data).

Example: “The results indicate a significant positive relationship between structured online learning and academic performance.”

Conclusion / Implications​

Explains the significance or implications of the findings.

Example: “These findings suggest that well-designed online learning environments can enhance student performance.”

Types of Abstracts​

Descriptive Abstract​

  • Summarizes the topic and scope
  • Does not include results or conclusions
  • Common in humanities and theoretical papers

Example: “This paper discusses the role of online learning in higher education and explores factors influencing student engagement.”

Informative Abstract​

  • Includes purpose, methods, results, and conclusions
  • Most common in science, engineering, and social sciences

Sample Abstract​

Online learning has become increasingly prevalent in higher education, raising questions about its effectiveness in improving student outcomes. This study investigates the impact of online learning on the academic performance of university students. A quantitative research approach was employed, and data were collected from 300 undergraduate students using a structured questionnaire. Statistical analysis was conducted using regression techniques. The results reveal that online learning has a significant positive effect on students’ academic performance, particularly when interactive tools and timely feedback are incorporated. The study concludes that effective implementation of online learning strategies can enhance academic achievement and recommends that universities invest in faculty training and digital infrastructure.

Explanation of the Example

Abstract ComponentExplanation
BackgroundIntroduces online learning and its relevance
ObjectiveStates the purpose of the study
MethodsDescribes research design, sample, and analysis
ResultsSummarizes main findings
ConclusionHighlights implications and recommendations

Language and Style Guidelines​

  • Write in past tense (methods and results)
  • Use third person and an objective tone
  • Avoid citations and references
  • Avoid abbreviations unless widely known
  • Do not include tables, figures, or equations

Common Mistakes to Avoid​

  • Including too much background information
  • Writing vague or general statements
  • Adding information not present in the paper
  • Using undefined abbreviations
  • Repeating the introduction verbatim

Step-by-Step Guide to Writing an Abstract​

  1. Identify the research problem
  2. State the objective clearly
  3. Briefly describe methods
  4. Highlight key results
  5. State conclusions and implications
  6. Revise to meet word limits

Final Tip​

A strong abstract should answer these questions:

What was done? Why was it done? How was it done? What was found? Why does it matter?

Question Should Asked​

Background and Research Problem​

  • What broader scholarly or practical context does this study address?
  • Is the research problem or gap clearly and succinctly stated?
  • Have I avoided unnecessary literature detail while still motivating the study?
  • Does the opening sentence orient readers from outside my narrow subfield?
  • Is the significance of the problem evident without overstatement?

Research Objectives and Questions​

  • Are the main research objective(s) explicitly stated?
  • Have I clearly articulated what the study seeks to examine, explain, test, or explore?
  • Do the objectives align precisely with the problem introduced?
  • For exploratory or qualitative studies, is the inquiry focus clearly conveyed?
  • Have I avoided vague aims such as “to investigate” without specifying what and why?

Theoretical or Conceptual Framework (if applicable)​

  • Is it necessary to mention the theoretical or conceptual lens in the abstract?
  • Does the abstract clarify whether the study is theory-building, theory-testing, or theory-applying?
  • Have I avoided naming theories that play only a minor role in the study?
  • Is the level of abstraction appropriate for the abstract’s word limit?
  • Does the theoretical framing enhance clarity rather than burden the abstract?

Methodological Approach​

  • Have I clearly identified the overall research design (quantitative, qualitative, mixed-methods)?
  • Is the methodological approach described with appropriate specificity?
  • For qualitative studies, is the nature of inquiry (e.g., interviews, ethnography) evident?
  • For quantitative studies, is the study design (e.g., experimental, cross-sectional) clear?
  • For mixed-methods studies, is integration or sequencing clearly implied?

Data, Sample, or Study Context​

  • Is the data source, sample, or empirical context clearly identified?
  • Have I included essential characteristics (e.g., sample size, population, setting) without excessive detail?
  • Is the geographic, institutional, or temporal context specified where relevant?
  • For secondary data or archival studies, is the data origin clear?
  • Does the abstract avoid misleading generalizations beyond the sample or context?

Analytical or Procedural Methods​

  • Have I clearly indicated how the data were analyzed?
  • Is the level of methodological detail appropriate for an abstract?
  • For quantitative studies, are key analytical techniques named accurately?
  • For qualitative studies, is the analytical approach (e.g., thematic analysis) evident?
  • Have I avoided technical jargon that would obscure meaning for non-specialists?

Key Results or Findings​

  • Are the main findings clearly and explicitly stated?
  • Have I prioritized the most important results rather than listing many minor ones?
  • Are results presented as outcomes rather than promises or intentions?
  • For qualitative research, are insights or themes clearly summarized?
  • For quantitative research, are the direction and nature of effects conveyed without unnecessary statistics?

Interpretation and Implications​

  • Does the abstract clearly state why the findings matter?
  • Have I articulated theoretical, methodological, or practical implications concisely?
  • For applied research, are implications for policy, practice, or intervention evident?
  • Have I avoided overstating the significance or generalizability of the findings?
  • Is the interpretation consistent with the results presented?

Originality and Contribution​

  • Does the abstract clearly signal what is novel or distinctive about the study?
  • Have I explained how the study advances knowledge, theory, or practice?
  • Is the contribution framed in relation to existing research rather than in isolation?
  • Have I avoided unsubstantiated claims of originality?
  • Would a reviewer quickly understand the study’s value from the abstract alone?

Limitations and Boundaries (if appropriate)​

  • Is it necessary or expected by the journal to mention key limitations?
  • Have I indicated constraints without undermining the study’s contribution?
  • Are the boundaries of interpretation clear?
  • Have I avoided introducing limitations that are not discussed in the paper?
  • Is the balance between transparency and conciseness appropriate?

Coherence, Flow, and Conciseness​

  • Does the abstract follow a logical progression from problem to contribution?
  • Is each sentence necessary and non-redundant?
  • Have I respected the journal’s word limit without sacrificing clarity?
  • Does the abstract read as a unified narrative rather than disconnected sections?
  • Could a reader summarize the entire paper accurately after reading the abstract?

Alignment with Journal Guidelines​

  • Does the abstract comply with the journal’s format (structured vs. unstructured)?
  • Have I used tense and voice consistent with journal norms?
  • Does the abstract reflect the disciplinary expectations of the target journal?
  • Have I avoided content prohibited by the journal (e.g., citations, abbreviations)?
  • Is the abstract consistent with the title and keywords?

Indexing, Keywords, and Discoverability​

  • Have I naturally incorporated key terms used in the field?
  • Would the abstract perform well in academic search engines and databases?
  • Have I avoided overly creative phrasing that reduces discoverability?
  • Are central concepts clearly named rather than implied?
  • Does the abstract support accurate indexing in Scopus or Web of Science?

Final Editorial Self-Check​

  • Does the abstract accurately represent the full paper?
  • Would a reviewer consider the abstract complete and informative?
  • Have I avoided claims not supported in the manuscript?
  • Is the abstract accessible to readers outside my immediate specialization?
  • Would I select this paper for review based on the abstract alone?
