Markov Model

A Markov model is a mathematical model used to describe systems that move between different states over time, where the key idea is the Markov property:

The future state depends only on the current state — not on how the system got there.

Video Explaination

Core Idea (Markov Property)​

If a system is in state ( StS_tSt​ ), then:

P(St+1∣St,St−1,...,S0)=P(St+1∣St)P(S_{t+1} \mid S_t, S_{t-1}, ..., S_0) = P(S_{t+1} \mid S_t)P(St+1​∣St​,St−1​,...,S0​)=P(St+1​∣St​)

This means:

  • No memory of the past
  • Only the present matters for predicting the future

Components of a Markov Model​

A Markov model usually includes:

1. States​

All possible conditions the system can be in.

Example: Weather = {Sunny, Rainy, Cloudy}

2. Transition Probabilities​

Probability of moving from one state to another

From/ToSunnyRainySunny0.80.2Rainy0.40.6\begin{matrix} From / To & Sunny & Rainy \\ Sunny & 0.8 & 0.2 \\ Rainy & 0.4 & 0.6 \\ \end{matrix}From/ToSunnyRainy​Sunny0.80.4​Rainy0.20.6​

3. Transition Matrix​

P=[0.80.2 0.40.6]P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.2 \ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix}P=[0.8​0.2 0.4​0.6​]

Types of Markov Models​

1. Discrete-Time Markov Chain (DTMC)​

  • Time moves in steps (t = 1, 2, 3...)
  • Most common type

2. Continuous-Time Markov Chain (CTMC)​

  • Time is continuous

3. Hidden Markov Model (HMM)​

  • States are not directly observable
  • Used in speech recognition, NLP

Example: Weather Prediction​

Let’s build a simple Markov model.

States:​

  • Sunny (S)
  • Rainy (R)

Transition Probabilities:​

  • Sunny → Sunny = 0.7
  • Sunny → Rainy = 0.3
  • Rainy → Sunny = 0.4
  • Rainy → Rainy = 0.6

Step 1: Transition Matrix​

P=[0.70.30.40.6]P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix}P=[0.70.4​0.30.6​]

Step 2: Initial State​

Suppose today is Sunny:

π0=[1,0]\pi_0 = [1, 0]π0​=[1,0]

Step 3: Predict Tomorrow​

π1=π0⋅P\pi_1 = \pi_0 \cdot Pπ1​=π0​⋅P π1=[1,0]⋅[0.70.30.40.6]=[0.7,0.3]\pi_1 = [1, 0] \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 0.7 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix} = [0.7, 0.3]π1​=[1,0]⋅[0.70.4​0.30.6​]=[0.7,0.3]

Interpretation:

  • 70% chance Sunny tomorrow
  • 30% chance Rainy tomorrow

Step 4: Predict Day After Tomorrow​

π2=π1⋅P\pi_2 = \pi_1 \cdot Pπ2​=π1​⋅P π2=[0.7,0.3]⋅P=[0.61,0.39]\pi_2 = [0.7, 0.3] \cdot P = [0.61, 0.39]π2​=[0.7,0.3]⋅P=[0.61,0.39]

Interpretation:

  • 61% Sunny
  • 39% Rainy

Real-Life Applications​

Markov models are widely used in:

  1. Google Search & Ranking: PageRank algorithm (based on Markov chains)
  2. Speech Recognition: Hidden Markov Models (HMM)
  3. Finance: Stock price modeling
  4. Biology: DNA sequence analysis
  5. Games & AI: Decision making in uncertain environments

Key Properties​

  1. Memoryless: Only current state matters
  2. Probabilistic: Transitions are based on probability
  3. State-based: System is always in one defined state

Simple Intuition​

Think of it like this:

If today is rainy, the chance of tomorrow being sunny depends only on today’s weather, not the entire past week.

Bonus: Steady-State (Long-Term Behavior)​

After many steps, probabilities stabilize.

We solve:

πP=π\pi P = \piπP=π

This gives long-term probabilities of being in each state.

Summary​

  • A Markov model describes transitions between states
  • It follows the memoryless property
  • Defined by a transition matrix
  • Used for prediction, modeling uncertainty, and AI systems
