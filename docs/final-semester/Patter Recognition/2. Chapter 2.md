## Human Eye
* The eye is a **biological imaging system**
* Retina acts as a **sensor array**
* Brain performs **pattern recognition**
* Many image processing algorithms are inspired by:
  * Visual perception
  * Retinal signal processing
  * Spatial and color sensitivity

###  Structural Components of the Eye

The human eye functions similarly to a **digital camera system**:

* **Lens system** → focuses light
* **Sensor array (retina)** → captures image
* **Optic nerve & brain** → processes and interprets patterns

![Eye](https://harvardeye.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/Diagram-of-the-Eye.png)

#### 1. Cornea

* Transparent outer surface of the eye
* Provides **most of the eye’s focusing power (~65–75%)**
* Fixed curvature (does not adjust like the lens)

**Image Processing Relevance:**

* Acts like a **fixed optical filter**
* Introduces **refraction and slight distortion**
* Comparable to **pre-processing optics** in cameras

#### 2. Aqueous Humor

* Fluid between cornea and lens
* Maintains pressure and provides nutrients

**Relevance:**

* Maintains **optical clarity**
* Analogous to maintaining **noise-free transmission medium**

#### 3. Iris and Pupil

* **Iris**: controls the size of the pupil
* **Pupil**: opening through which light enters  
  Equivalent to aperture control in cameras:
  - Large pupil → more light (low-light conditions)
  - Small pupil → less light (bright conditions)

**Function:**

* Regulates light intensity entering the eye

**Image Processing Analogy:**

* Equivalent to **aperture control (f-stop)** in cameras
* Controls:
  * Brightness
  * Depth of field
  * Signal-to-noise ratio

#### 4. Lens

* Flexible, biconvex structure
* Changes shape to focus (accommodation)

**Function:**

* Focuses light onto the retina

**Image Processing Relevance:**

* Acts like a **variable-focus lens**
* Responsible for:
  * Image sharpness
  * Blur (defocus)
* Related to:
  * **Point Spread Function (PSF)**
  * **Image blurring models**

#### 5. Vitreous Humor

* Gel-like substance filling the eye

**Function:**

* Maintains eye shape
* Allows light transmission

**Relevance:**

* Acts as a **transparent propagation medium**
* Minimal effect but can introduce **scattering noise** in aging eyes

#### 6. Retina (Most Important for Image Processing)

The **retina** is the biological equivalent of an **image sensor**.

**Structure:**

* Multi-layered neural tissue
* Contains **photoreceptors**

**Types of Photoreceptors:**

1. Rod Cells

    * ~120 million
    * Sensitive to **low light (scotopic vision)**
    * Do not detect color  

    **Relevance:**  

    * High sensitivity → **noise-tolerant detection**  
    * Used as inspiration for:
        * Low-light imaging
        * Edge detection under poor illumination
2. Cone Cells
    * ~6–7 million
    * Responsible for **color vision (RGB-like)**
    * Operate in bright light

    **Types:**

    * L-cones (Red)
    * M-cones (Green)
    * S-cones (Blue)

    **Relevance:**

    * Basis for **RGB color models**
    * Used in:
      * Color image processing
      * Feature extraction

#### 7. Optic Nerve

* Transmits signals from retina to brain

**Relevance:**

* Comparable to **data transmission channel**
* Limited bandwidth → leads to:
  * **Data compression in biological vision**

### Image formation in Human Eye

![Image Formation in Human Eye](/img/image_processing/image_formation_in_human_eye.png)

1. Light enters through cornea
2. Passes through pupil (controlled by iris)
3. Focused by lens
4. Forms **inverted image on retina**
5. Converted into electrical signals
6. Sent to brain for interpretation

```
Object → Cornea → Vitreous Humor → Pupil → Lens → Retina → Photoreceptors → Optic Nerve → Brain
```

**Eye vs Digital Image Processing System**

| Eye Component | Image Processing Equivalent      |
| ------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Cornea + Lens | Optical system (lens)            |
| Pupil         | Aperture                         |
| Retina        | Image sensor (CCD/CMOS)          |
| Rods & Cones  | Pixels / color sensors           |
| Optic nerve   | Data transmission                |
| Brain         | CPU / pattern recognition system |

## Brightness

> Illumination refers to the amount of light falling on a surface.  
> It describes how much light reaches an object, not how much is reflected.

**Importance of Brightness**

1. Image Enhancement
    * Histogram equalization mimics adaptation by redistributing intensities
    * Contrast stretching improves discrimination
2. Camera Systems
    * Auto-exposure = artificial brightness adaptation
    * HDR imaging = expands discrimination range
3. Pattern Recognition
    * Feature extraction depends on contrast sensitivity
    * Edge detection (Sobel, Canny) relies on brightness discrimination
4. Compression
    * JPEG exploits limited human discrimination (lossy compression)


### Brightness Adaptation

**Brightness adaptation** is the ability of the visual system to adjust its sensitivity depending on the **overall illumination level** in the environment.

* In bright sunlight → eye becomes less sensitive
* In dim light → eye becomes more sensitive

This allows humans to operate over an extremely large range of illumination ($≈ (10^{-6}) to (10^{6}) cd/m²$), far beyond any camera sensor in a single exposure.

#### Mechanism (Biological + Perceptual View)

Brightness adaptation occurs mainly due to:

1. Pupil adjustment
    * Pupil constricts in bright light → reduces light entering eye
    * Pupil dilates in darkness → increases light entry
2. Photoreceptor adaptation (main factor)
    * **Rods** (low light vision) and **cones** (daylight color vision) adjust sensitivity chemically.
    * Photopigments (rhodopsin in rods) regenerate at different rates depending on light exposure.
3. Neural adaptation
    * Retina and visual cortex adjust gain (signal amplification).

#### Brightness Adaptation Curve

A key concept is the **logarithmic response of perceived brightness**:

$$
B_p = k \log(1 + I)
$$

Where:

* ($B_p$) = perceived brightness
* ($I$) = physical intensity
* ($k$) = scaling constant

#### Adaptation Levels (Dynamic Range)

The eye effectively “shifts” its working range depending on background illumination:

* In darkness → sensitive to small intensity changes
* In daylight → sensitive to high intensities

This shifting is called **light adaptation (photopic)** and **dark adaptation (scotopic)**.

### Brightness Discrimination

**Brightness discrimination** is the ability to distinguish between **two similar brightness levels**.

* Can you tell the difference between gray level 120 and 125?

This is closely tied to **contrast sensitivity**.

#### Weber’s Law (Core Principle)

Brightness discrimination follows **Weber’s Law**:

$$
\frac{\Delta I}{I} = k
$$

Where:

* ($\Delta I$) = minimum detectable change in intensity
* ($I$) = background intensity
* ($k$) = Weber fraction (approximately constant over a range)

**Apply Weber's Law**

Now inside the dark room:

You look at two gray patches:

* Patch A: intensity = 10
* Patch B: intensity = 11

$$
\frac{\Delta I}{I} = k
$$

Assume: $k = 0.1$

Compute:

$$
\Delta I = k \cdot I = 0.1 \times 10 = 1
$$

> Weber’s Law predicts the minimum detectable change from the baseline(starting intensity).

Minimum noticeable difference = 1

Since:

* Actual difference = 1

You **can detect the difference**

#### Interpretation

* At higher brightness → you need a larger absolute change to notice a difference
* At lower brightness → even small changes are noticeable

| Background Intensity (I) | Just Noticeable Difference (ΔI) |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Dark scene (10)          | ~1                              |
| Medium (100)             | ~10                             |
| Bright (1000)            | ~100                            |

So perception is **relative, not absolute**.

## Digital Image Formation

In **image processing and pattern recognition**, *sampling* and *quantization* are the two fundamental steps used to convert a **continuous real-world image** into a **digital image** that a computer can process. Together, they define how an image is represented in discrete form.

A real-world image is a **continuous function**:

$$
f(x, y)
$$

Where:

* ($x, y$) = spatial coordinates (continuous)
* ($f(x, y)$) = intensity (brightness or color)

To store this in a computer, we must convert it into a **digital image** using:

1. **Sampling → discretize space (x, y)**
2. **Quantization → discretize intensity values**

**Importance of Sampling and Quantization**

1. Image Quality
    * Sampling controls sharpness and resolution
    * Quantization controls smoothness and color fidelity
2. Feature Extraction
    * Edge detection depends on sampling density
    * Gradient computation depends on quantization precision
3. Computer Vision
    * Object recognition accuracy depends on both:
      * Enough pixels (sampling)
      * Enough intensity detail (quantization)
4. Compression
    * JPEG reduces spatial redundancy (sampling-related)
    * Also reduces intensity precision (quantization)

> Sampling defines **where information is taken**  
> Quantization defines **how precisely it is represented**

### Image Sampling

**Sampling** is the process of converting a continuous image into a **finite set of discrete spatial points (pixels)**.

#### Mathematical Representation

We sample the continuous image ($f(x, y)$) at regular intervals:

$$
f(x, y) \rightarrow f(i\Delta x, j\Delta y)
$$

Where:

* ($i, j$) = integers (pixel indices)
* ($\Delta x, \Delta y$) = sampling intervals

Result: A **grid of pixels**

#### Sampling Rate

Sampling rate determines **spatial resolution**:

* High sampling rate → more pixels → finer detail
* Low sampling rate → fewer pixels → coarse image

### Image Quantization

**Quantization** is the process of mapping continuous intensity values into a **finite set of discrete gray levels**.

> Quantization decides “how many brightness levels” are allowed.

#### Mathematical Representation

Original intensity:

$$
f(x, y) \in [0, L_{max}]
$$

Quantized intensity:

$$
f_q(x, y) \in {0, 1, 2, ..., L-1}
$$

Where:

* ($L$) = number of gray levels (e.g., 256)

#### Bit Depth

Quantization depends on bit depth:

| Bit depth | Gray levels (L) |
| --------- | --------------- |
| 1-bit     | 2 (black/white) |
| 4-bit     | 16 levels       |
| 8-bit     | 256 levels      |
| 16-bit    | 65,536 levels   |

#### Quantization Process

Steps:

1. Measure intensity at sampled pixel
2. Map it to nearest allowed level
3. Round off value

Example:

* Original intensity = 120.7
* Quantized (8-bit) = 121

#### Quantization Error

Error:

$$
e(x,y) = f(x,y) - f_q(x,y)
$$

This error is called: **quantization noise**

#### Effect of Low Quantization

When levels are too few:

* Banding effect appears (smooth gradients become steps)
* Loss of detail
* Poor color reproduction

Example: Sky gradient looks like stripes instead of smooth transition

#### Quantizing a Grayscale Image Pixel

Assume:

* Original intensity range:
  $$
  f(x,y) \in [0, 255]
  $$
* You reduce it to **4-bit image** →
  $$
  L = 16 \text{ levels}
  $$

1. Define Quantization Levels

    Each level interval:

    $$
    \Delta = \frac{256}{16} = 16
    $$

    So allowed values are approximately:

    ```
    0, 16, 32, 48, ..., 240
    ```

2. Apply Quantization Formula

    General formula:

    $$
    f_q(x,y) = \text{round}\left(\frac{f(x,y)}{\Delta}\right)
    $$

3. Example Calculation

    **Given pixel:**

    $$
    f(x,y) = 120.7
    $$

    Compute:

    $$
    f_q = \text{round}\left(\frac{120.7}{16}\right)
    = \text{round}(7.54)
    = 8
    $$

    Now map back to intensity:

    $$
    f_q(x,y) = 8 \times 16 = 128
    $$

4. Quantization Error

    $$
    e(x,y) = f(x,y) - f_q(x,y)
    $$

    $$
    e = 120.7 - 128 = -7.3
    $$

    This is **quantization noise**

5. Apply to Multiple Pixels (Mini Image)  
    Original image:
    ```
    [ 50   120.7   200 ]
    [ 30    90     180 ]
    ```
    After quantization (4-bit):
    ```
    [ 48   128   192 ]
    [ 32    96   176 ]
    ```
6. Observe the Effect
    * Values are **rounded to nearest level**
    * Smooth variation becomes **step-like**
    * This causes **banding**

### Sampling vs Quantization

| Feature          | Sampling           | Quantization         |
| ---------------- | ------------------ | -------------------- |
| What it controls | Spatial resolution | Intensity resolution |
| Domain           | x, y coordinates   | Brightness values    |
| Output           | Pixels arrangement | Pixel values         |
| Error type       | Aliasing           | Quantization noise   |

### Final Digital Image Representation

A digital image is formed by both:

1. Sampling: Convert continuous space → grid of pixels
2. Quantization: Convert continuous intensity → discrete levels

```
Continuous scene
      ↓
Sampling (pixel grid)
      ↓
Quantization (gray levels)
      ↓
Digital image matrix
```


## Color Perception

### Color Depends on Reflected Light

> *“The colors perceived in an object are determined by the nature of light reflected from the object.“*

* Objects don’t have color by themselves
* They **reflect certain wavelengths** and absorb others

Example:

* A red apple reflects **red wavelengths (~700 nm)**
* Absorbs blue and green

So your eyes perceive it as **red**

### Why Objects Appear White

> *“A body that reflects light in all visible wavelengths appears white.”*

* White objects reflect **almost all wavelengths equally**

Example:

* A white paper under sunlight reflects: Red + Green + Blue
* Your brain interprets this combination as **white**

### Why Objects Appear Green

> *“Green objects reflect wavelengths in [500–570 nm]”*

* Green color corresponds to:
  $$
  \lambda = 500 \text{ to } 570 \text{ nm}
  $$

Example:

* A leaf:
  * Reflects green light
  * Absorbs red and blue

So it appears **green**

### Achromatic / Monochromatic Light

**Achromatic Light**

* No color (only intensity)
* Includes:
  * Black
  * White
  * Gray

**Monochromatic Light**

* Light of **single wavelength**
* Pure color

Example: Laser light (pure red or green)

### Three Important Quantities

#### Radiance

* Total energy emitted by a light source
* Measured in **watts**

**Example**: A 100W bulb emits more radiance than a 40W bulb

#### Luminance

- How much light is **perceived by the eye**
- Depends on **human vision sensitivity**  

**Example**: Green light looks brighter than blue light even if both have same energy

#### Brightness

* **Subjective feeling** of light intensity
* Cannot be measured exactly

Example:

* Same screen:

  * Looks bright in dark room
  * Looks dim in sunlight

## Image Acquisition Using Sensors

**Image acquisition** is the process of capturing a real-world scene and converting it into a **digital image** using an **optical system + sensor + digitization electronics**.

> It is the bridge between the physical world (continuous signals) and digital image processing systems.

```
Scene → Illumination → Lens (Optics) → Sensor Array → Analog Signal → ADC → Digital Image
```

### Main Components of Image Acquisition System

#### 1. Scene (Object of Interest)

* Real-world object or environment
* Reflects or emits light
* Contains spatial and intensity information

Example: A face, document, road scene, medical scan

#### 2. Illumination System

* Provides light source (natural or artificial)
* Essential because sensors detect light, not objects directly

Types:

* Natural light (sunlight)
* Artificial light (LED, halogen, X-ray in medical imaging)

#### 3. Optical System (Lens)

**Function:** Focuses light from scene onto sensor plane.

**Key properties:**

* Focal length
* Aperture (controls brightness)
* Field of view

**Image formation principle:**

Based on **geometric optics**:

$$
\text{Light rays from object} \rightarrow \text{Lens focusing} \rightarrow \text{Image plane}
$$

**Important effects:**

* Blurring (defocus)
* Distortion (barrel, pincushion)
* Depth of field variation

#### 4. Image Sensor (Core Component)

**Function:** Converts **light energy (photons)** into **electrical signals**.

**Types of sensors:**

1. CCD (Charge-Coupled Device)
    * High image quality
    * Low noise
    * Slower readout
    * Used in scientific imaging
2. CMOS (Complementary Metal Oxide Semiconductor)
    * Faster
    * Low power
    * Cheap
    * Used in smartphones, webcams

#### 5. Sensor Structure (Pixel Array)

Sensor contains a grid of **photosensitive elements (pixels)**:

```
[● ● ● ●]
[● ● ● ●]
[● ● ● ●]
```

Each pixel:

* Captures light intensity
* Produces analog voltage proportional to light

#### 6. Photodetection Principle

Each pixel works based on:

**Photoelectric effect:**

$$
\text{Photons} \rightarrow \text{Electron generation}
$$

* More light → more electrons
* Less light → fewer electrons

Output signal:
$$
V(x, y) \propto \text{Light intensity}
$$

#### 7. Color Image Sensors

**Bayer Filter Pattern (most common)**

Each pixel captures only one color: Red, Green, Blue

Arrangement:

```
G R G R
B G B G
G R G R
B G B G
```

Then **demosaicing** reconstructs full RGB image.

#### 8. Analog Signal Processing

After light conversion:

* Pixel outputs are **analog voltages**
* These signals are amplified and filtered

Operations:

* Noise reduction
* Gain control (brightness adjustment)

#### 9. Analog-to-Digital Conversion (ADC)

**Function:** Converts analog signals into digital values.

Two steps involved:

* Sampling (spatial discretization already done by pixel grid)
* Quantization (intensity discretization)

**Output**: Digital pixel values (e.g., 0–255 for 8-bit image)

**Complete Image Acquisition Pipeline**

1. Scene emits/reflects light
2. Lens focuses light onto sensor
3. Sensor converts photons → electrical charges
4. Signal amplification & filtering
5. ADC converts analog → digital values
6. Image stored as matrix of pixels

Final output:

$$
f(x, y) \rightarrow f(i, j)
$$

### Types of Image Acquisition Systems

#### 1. Single Sensor System

* One detector + mechanical scanning
* Slow but high precision

Used in: Early scanners

#### 2. Line Sensor System

* One row of sensors
* Object moves or sensor scans

Used in:

* Flatbed scanners
* Satellite imaging

#### 3. Area Sensor System

* 2D array of sensors (most common)
* Captures full image at once

Used in:

* Cameras
* Smartphones
* Medical imaging devices

### Key Physical Principles

#### 1. Light Intensity Capture

$$
I(x,y) = \text{reflectance} \times \text{illumination}
$$

#### 2. Lens Equation

$$
\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}
$$

Controls image sharpness and focus.

**Noise in Image Acquisition**

Real sensors introduce noise:

1. Sources of Noise
    * Thermal noise
    * Shot noise (photon variation)
    * Electronic noise
    * Quantization noise
2. Effect
    * Grainy images
    * Loss of fine details

**Importance of Image Acquisition**

Image acquisition determines the **maximum possible quality of all downstream processing**.

1. Impact on Image Processing
    * Resolution depends on sensor density
    * Contrast depends on sensor dynamic range
    * Noise affects filtering and enhancement
2. Impact on Pattern Recognition
    * Poor acquisition → poor feature extraction
    * Edge detection accuracy depends on sensor quality
    * Object recognition depends on signal fidelity

Image acquisition using sensors is a multi-stage process where:

* Light from a scene is focused by a lens
* Converted into electrical signals by a sensor array
* Processed and digitized into a pixel matrix

> Sensors act as the physical interface between the real world and digital image processing systems.
