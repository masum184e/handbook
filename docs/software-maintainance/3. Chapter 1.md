- **Evolution** is a process of continuous change from a lower, simpler, or worse to a higher, more complex, or better state.
- **Maintainability** is the ease with which maintenance can be carried out.
- **Maintenance** is the act of keeping an entity in an existing state of repair, efficiency, or validity; to preserve from failure or decline.
- **Software** is the programs, documentation and operating procedures by which computers can be made useful to man.

## Why software maintainance needed

Software is rarely "done" after it is released. Maintenance is required for several critical reasons:

### A. Correcting Hidden Faults (The "Bug" Factor)
No matter how rigorous the testing phase is, users will always find edge cases that the developers didn't anticipate. Maintenance is needed to fix these bugs before they cause data loss or system failure.

### B. Environmental Adaptation
Software does not live in a vacuum. It sits on top of layers of other technology:
* **Hardware Upgrades:** Moving from older servers to modern cloud infrastructure.
* **Operating Systems:** An update to Windows, macOS, or Android can break existing software features.
* **Third-Party APIs:** If your app uses Google Maps or a payment gateway, and they change their code, your software must change too.

### C. Meeting Evolving User Needs
As users interact with a system, they discover new ways it could be more helpful. Maintenance allows for **perfective** changes—adding features or simplifying the interface—to ensure the software remains competitive and useful.

### D. Security and Vulnerability Management
Cybersecurity threats evolve daily. Maintenance is required to patch security holes and update encryption methods to protect user data from new types of attacks.

## Relationship between SDLC and Software Maintainance

The **Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)** and **Software Maintenance** are often viewed as two separate phases, but in modern engineering, they are deeply intertwined.

### The Traditional View: Maintenance as the "Final Stage"
In the classic Waterfall model, Maintenance is the **final phase** of the SDLC. 
1.  Requirements $\rightarrow$ Design $\rightarrow$ Implementation $\rightarrow$ Testing $\rightarrow$ Deployment.
2.  **Maintenance** begins only after Deployment is complete.
3.  In this view, maintenance is a "loop" that repeats the previous SDLC steps (Requirements, Design, Coding) on a smaller scale for every change.

### The Modern View: The Continuous Cycle
In Agile and DevOps environments, the line between "Development" and "Maintenance" has almost disappeared.

| Feature | SDLC (Development) | Software Maintenance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Focus** | Creating the initial "Base" architecture. | Evolving and protecting that architecture. |
| **Duration** | Usually months or a few years. | Can last **10 to 30+ years** (the longest phase). |
| **Inputs** | Original Business Requirements. | User Feedback, Bug Reports, and Tech Changes. |

### Key Connections
* **The "SDLC-in-Maintenance":** Every time a maintenance request is approved, a **mini-SDLC** occurs. You must analyze the requirements of the change, design the fix, code it, test it (Regression Testing), and redeploy it.
* **Maintainability starts in the SDLC:** The quality of the **Design and Implementation** phases of the initial SDLC determines how expensive the Maintenance phase will be. If the SDLC is rushed and produces "spaghetti code," maintenance costs will skyrocket.
* **Feedback Loop:** Maintenance provides the data (error logs and user frustrations) that informs the requirements for the **next version** of the SDLC.

> **The Bottom Line:** If the SDLC is the "birth" of the software, Maintenance is its "adult life." One cannot exist effectively without the other.

## Categorising Software Change

Software change may be needed, and initiated, for a number of reasons and can be categorised accordingly. In brief, software change may be classified under the following categories:

- **Modification initiated by defects in the software.**
- **Change driven by the need to accommodate modifications in the environment of the software system.**
- **Change undertaken to expand the existing requirements of a system.**
- **Change undertaken to prevent malfunctions.**

The categorising of software change is not simply an academic exercise. It is vital in understanding when and how to make changes, how to assign resources, and how to prioritise requests for change. These areas are expanded and explained in more detail in the following chapters.

<details>

<summary>Define the term software maintenance and indicate the factors that initiate the modification of software.</summary>


**Software Maintenance** is the process of modifying a software system after it has been delivered to the customer. Its primary objectives are to correct faults, improve performance or other attributes, and adapt the system to a changed environment. It is not merely "fixing bugs" but an ongoing evolution to ensure the software remains useful and functional over its lifetime.

**Factors that Initiate Software Modification**

Several factors trigger the need for changes in a software system:

* **Error Correction:** Identifying and fixing coding, design, or requirements errors (bugs) discovered by users during operation.
* **Environmental Changes:** Adapting the software to new operating systems, hardware upgrades, or changes in third-party libraries and platforms.
* **User Requirements:** Requests from stakeholders for new features, functional enhancements, or improvements to the user interface.
* **Performance Optimization:** Refining the code to improve execution speed, reduce memory consumption, or enhance overall efficiency.
* **Regulatory/Legal Changes:** Modifications required to comply with new laws, such as data privacy acts or tax regulation updates.
* **Preventative Measures:** Proactively updating the system to prevent future failures or to make the software more maintainable (often called software re-engineering).

<details>

<summary>Compare and contrast software development and software maintenance.</summary>

While software development and maintenance share similar technical activities (coding, testing, documenting), they differ significantly in their constraints and objectives.

| Feature | Software Development | Software Maintenance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Starting Point** | Begins with a "blank slate" based on new requirements. | Begins with an existing, complex codebase. |
| **Constraints** | Limited by project scope, budget, and timeline. | Limited by the **existing architecture** and design choices. |
| **Objective** | To create a functional product from scratch. | To preserve or enhance value while ensuring system stability. |
| **Testing** | Focuses on verifying new features against specifications. | Focuses on **regression testing** to ensure changes didn't break old features. |
| **Risk** | Risk of failing to meet initial requirements or deadlines. | Risk of "ripple effects" where a small change causes unexpected failures elsewhere. |
| **User Feedback** | Based on anticipated needs or prototypes. | Based on real-world usage and concrete error reports. |

**Key Contrast: The "Ripple Effect"**

In development, adding a new module is relatively isolated. In maintenance, every change must be carefully evaluated for its impact on the rest of the system. Because the original developers may no longer be available, maintainers often spend more time **comprehending** the existing code than actually writing new lines of code.
